# Framework: Market Research Synthesiser

Defines the methodology for consolidating primary and secondary research into a coherent strategic synthesis.

## Source Evaluation Matrix

### Source Quality Tiers

| Tier | Source Type | Reliability | Recency Requirement |
|------|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| T1 | Primary qualitative (interviews, ethnography, usability studies) | High for "why" | <18 months |
| T1 | Primary quantitative (surveys n≥100, experiments, A/B tests) | High for "what/how many" | <12 months |
| T2 | Secondary analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, CB Insights) | Medium-High | <24 months |
| T2 | Industry association surveys and benchmarks | Medium | <18 months |
| T3 | Academic research | High for frameworks | Methodology > recency |
| T3 | Internal analytics and product data | High for internal behaviour | <6 months for trend use |
| T4 | Press coverage, blog posts, social sentiment | Low | <3 months for signal use |

### Conflict Resolution Rules

When sources disagree:
1. Higher-tier source wins over lower-tier
2. More recent wins over older (same tier)
3. Larger sample size wins (same tier, same recency)
4. If conflict persists after rules: document both signals; label as "contested finding"; recommend primary research to resolve

## Market Sizing Methods

| Method | When to Use | Inputs Required |
|--------|------------|----------------|
| Top-down (TAM → SAM → SOM) | High-level market validation; investor materials | Analyst market size estimates, company's addressable segment definition |
| Bottom-up (unit × price × penetration) | Operational planning; more defensible | Unit count by segment, conversion rates, ACV |
| Value theory (WTP × segment size) | Pricing decisions; premium positioning | Willingness-to-pay research, segment size |
| Comparable transaction | M&A context; comparable company sizing | Comparable company metrics, multiple benchmarks |

## Synthesis Themes

### Standard Market Synthesis Dimensions

| Dimension | Key Questions | Typical Evidence Sources |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|
| Market size & growth | How large is the total opportunity? At what rate is it growing? | Analyst reports, bottom-up estimates |
| Customer segments | Who are the distinct buyer/user segments? What distinguishes them? | Customer interviews, CRM data, survey segmentation |
| Growth drivers | What macro or industry forces are accelerating demand? | Industry reports, customer interviews |
| Friction points | What barriers slow adoption or switching? | Customer interviews, churn analysis, support tickets |
| Competitive dynamics | Who are the main players and how is share distributed? | Competitive analysis, analyst quadrants, review sites |
| Unmet needs | What are customers trying to do that no current solution satisfies well? | JTBD research, support tickets, sales call analysis |

## Strategic Implication Categories

Each synthesis theme should map to one of these strategic implication types:

| Implication Type | Example | Urgency |
|-----------------|---------|---------|
| Market entry validation | "The unmet need in segment X is large enough to justify a dedicated product line" | High if investment imminent |
| Positioning adjustment | "Competitors are abandoning the 'ease of use' positioning; opportunity to own it" | Medium — 1-2 quarters |
| Roadmap prioritisation | "Growth driver Y will make capability Z a hygiene requirement within 18 months" | High if on current roadmap |
| Risk flag | "Market growth is concentrated in a segment the company is not currently serving" | Medium — track quarterly |
| Pricing signal | "Segment A has 2× higher WTP than segment B; current uniform pricing may be leaving revenue on the table" | Medium — pricing review |
