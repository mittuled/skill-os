# Framework: Meeting Prep Builder

The meeting prep builder uses an 11-section brief structure, adapted by meeting type, with a companion cheat sheet and question taxonomy.

## 11-Section Brief Structure

### 1. Company Snapshot
- Company name, industry, employee count, revenue (estimated or known), HQ location
- Funding stage and recent rounds (if applicable)
- Key products/services and market position
- Guidance: keep to 5-7 bullet points; AEs need context, not a Wikipedia entry

### 2. Attendee Profiles
- Name, title, LinkedIn summary, tenure at company
- Role in buying process (economic buyer, champion, influencer, blocker, end user)
- Communication style signals (formal/informal, data-driven/narrative-driven)
- Previous interactions with your team (meetings, emails, support tickets)
- Guidance: prioritize decision-makers; flag anyone new to the conversation

### 3. Deal History
- Current deal stage and pipeline value
- Timeline of key milestones (first touch, demo, proposal, stall points)
- Previous proposals or pricing shared
- Open action items from last interaction
- Guidance: highlight anything that has stalled or changed since last contact

### 4. Competitive Landscape
- Known competitors in the deal (mentioned by prospect or observed)
- Competitor strengths in this specific account context
- Your differentiation points relevant to this prospect's pain
- Guidance: reference battle cards from sales-competitive-intel if available

### 5. Recent News
- Company news from last 30 days (press releases, earnings, leadership changes)
- Industry news affecting the prospect's market
- Trigger events (hiring sprees, layoffs, M&A, regulatory changes)
- Guidance: only include news relevant to the conversation — not a news dump

### 6. Pain Points
- Confirmed pain points from discovery (with source: meeting notes, email, call)
- Hypothesized pain points based on industry and company profile
- Priority ranking: which pain is most acute and budget-worthy
- Guidance: distinguish confirmed from hypothesized; never present a guess as a fact

### 7. Value Propositions
- Mapped to each confirmed pain point: what your solution does about it
- Quantified where possible (time saved, cost reduced, revenue generated)
- Proof points: similar customers, case studies, benchmarks
- Guidance: lead with the value proposition most relevant to the primary attendee's role

### 8. Objection Prep
- Top 3-5 likely objections based on deal stage and prospect profile
- Prepared responses with evidence (not just rebuttals)
- Landmines: topics to avoid or handle carefully
- Guidance: include both product objections and commercial objections (price, timeline, risk)

### 9. Agenda Suggestion
- Proposed agenda with time allocations
- Key topics the AE wants to cover
- Topics the prospect is likely to raise
- Guidance: always include time for prospect questions; never fill the entire slot

### 10. Questions to Ask
- Discovery questions (open-ended, exploring unknowns)
- Validation questions (confirming assumptions)
- Advancement questions (moving the deal forward: next steps, timeline, decision process)
- Guidance: prioritize the single most important question; if you only get to ask one, make it count

### 11. Success Metrics
- What a successful meeting outcome looks like (specific next step)
- Fallback outcome if primary goal is not achievable
- Signals to watch for during the meeting (engagement, objections, body language cues)
- Guidance: define success before the meeting, not after

## Meeting Type Adaptations

### Discovery Meeting
- Heavy emphasis: sections 6 (Pain Points), 10 (Questions to Ask), 2 (Attendee Profiles)
- Light emphasis: sections 4 (Competitive Landscape), 8 (Objection Prep)
- Primary goal: understand the problem before presenting any solution
- Question ratio: 70% open/probing, 30% confirming

### Demo / Presentation
- Heavy emphasis: sections 7 (Value Propositions), 6 (Pain Points), 2 (Attendee Profiles)
- Light emphasis: sections 3 (Deal History), 5 (Recent News)
- Primary goal: demonstrate value tied to specific pain points
- Question ratio: 40% open, 60% confirming/validating

### Negotiation
- Heavy emphasis: sections 8 (Objection Prep), 3 (Deal History), 4 (Competitive Landscape)
- Light emphasis: sections 1 (Company Snapshot), 5 (Recent News)
- Primary goal: resolve objections and agree on commercial terms
- Question ratio: 30% open, 70% closed/confirming

### QBR / Renewal
- Heavy emphasis: sections 3 (Deal History), 7 (Value Propositions), 11 (Success Metrics)
- Light emphasis: sections 4 (Competitive Landscape), 10 (Questions to Ask)
- Primary goal: demonstrate ROI achieved and align on next period goals
- Question ratio: 50% confirming, 50% open (future needs)

## Cheat Sheet Format

One page, structured as:

```
MEETING CHEAT SHEET
Date: [date] | Account: [name] | Meeting Type: [type]

ATTENDEES
- [Name] — [Title] — [Role in deal] — [Key fact]
- [Name] — [Title] — [Role in deal] — [Key fact]

TOP 3 PAIN POINTS
1. [Pain] — [Evidence source]
2. [Pain] — [Evidence source]
3. [Pain] — [Evidence source]

THE ONE QUESTION
[The single most important question to answer in this meeting]

LANDMINES (avoid/handle carefully)
- [Topic] — [Why it's sensitive]

DESIRED NEXT STEP
[Specific, concrete next step to propose at meeting end]

FALLBACK NEXT STEP
[If primary next step is not achievable]
```

## Question Taxonomy

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Open | Explore unknowns, invite narrative | "Walk me through how your team handles X today." |
| Closed | Get specific data points | "How many users are on the platform today?" |
| Probing | Deepen understanding of a stated point | "You mentioned Y is a challenge — what does that cost you per quarter?" |
| Confirming | Validate assumptions or prior statements | "Last time we spoke, you said Z was the priority — is that still the case?" |
