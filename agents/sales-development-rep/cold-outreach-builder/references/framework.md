# Framework: Cold Outreach Sequence Design

This framework defines the outreach frameworks, cadence science, personalization hierarchy, and email structure patterns used by the cold-outreach-builder skill.

## Outreach Frameworks

### AIDA Framework — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

**Best for**: Unaware and problem-aware prospects who do not yet know your product or category exists.

**Structure per email**:

| Element | Purpose | Technique |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Attention** | Stop the scroll. Get the email opened. | Provocative subject line, surprising statistic, bold claim, or name-drop a peer company. |
| **Interest** | Earn the read. Make the problem feel personal. | Connect the opening hook to a specific pain the prospect's role experiences. Use "you" language, not "we" language. |
| **Desire** | Make the outcome tangible. | Paint a concrete before/after scenario. Quantify the improvement (time saved, revenue gained, risk reduced). |
| **Action** | One clear CTA. | Ask for one specific thing: 15-minute call, reply with availability, watch a 2-minute video. Never stack CTAs. |

**Email arc using AIDA across 5 emails**:

- **Email 1 (Attention)**: Open with a surprising data point or trend relevant to the prospect's industry. Establish that you understand their world. CTA: "Is this on your radar?"
- **Email 2 (Interest)**: Share a specific example of the problem costing a peer company time or money. Validate the pain. CTA: "Have you seen this in your team?"
- **Email 3 (Desire)**: Present the transformation — what "after" looks like. Use a brief case study or metric. CTA: "Worth a 15-minute conversation?"
- **Email 4 (Desire+)**: Deepen with a more detailed case study or third-party validation. Preempt the top objection. CTA: "Can I send you the full case study?"
- **Email 5 (Action)**: Breakup email. Acknowledge you may have missed the mark. Offer final value (resource, benchmark, insight). CTA: "If timing changes, here is how to reach me."

### PAS Framework — Problem, Agitate, Solve

**Best for**: Problem-aware prospects who know the pain but have not explored solutions.

**Structure per email**:

| Element | Purpose | Technique |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Problem** | Name the specific problem. | Be precise — not "sales is hard" but "your SDRs are spending 40% of their time on manual research instead of selling." |
| **Agitate** | Make the cost of inaction vivid. | Quantify the downstream impact: lost revenue, team burnout, competitive disadvantage, compliance risk. |
| **Solve** | Position your solution as the bridge. | Brief, specific, focused on the outcome. Not a feature list — a result statement. |

**Email arc using PAS across 5 emails**:

- **Email 1 (Problem)**: Name a specific, research-backed problem the prospect's role faces. CTA: "Is this costing your team time?"
- **Email 2 (Agitate)**: Quantify the compounding cost of the problem over time. Use industry benchmarks. CTA: "How does this compare to what you are seeing?"
- **Email 3 (Solve)**: Introduce the solution through a peer's result. Focus on the outcome, not the mechanism. CTA: "15 minutes to see if this applies?"
- **Email 4 (Social Proof)**: Stack multiple proof points — logos, metrics, quotes. Preempt the "sounds too good" objection. CTA: "Want me to connect you with [reference customer]?"
- **Email 5 (Breakup)**: Final value offer. Share a relevant resource regardless of whether they engage. CTA: "No reply needed — here is something useful either way."

### BAB Framework — Before, After, Bridge

**Best for**: Solution-aware prospects who are evaluating options and need to see the transformation clearly.

**Structure per email**:

| Element | Purpose | Technique |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Before** | Paint the current state vividly. | Describe the status quo in the prospect's own language. Use phrases from job postings, earnings calls, or industry forums. |
| **After** | Show the transformed state. | Concrete, specific, quantified. Not "better sales" but "2x reply rate and 40% reduction in ramp time." |
| **Bridge** | Explain how to get from Before to After. | Your product is the bridge. Keep it brief — one sentence on the mechanism, then back to outcomes. |

**Email arc using BAB across 5 emails**:

- **Email 1 (Before)**: Describe the status quo pain in vivid detail. Demonstrate deep understanding. CTA: "Sound familiar?"
- **Email 2 (After)**: Paint the transformed state with a customer example. Quantify the delta. CTA: "Worth exploring?"
- **Email 3 (Bridge)**: Briefly explain how the product bridges the gap. One mechanism sentence, then back to outcomes. CTA: "15 minutes to walk through it?"
- **Email 4 (Proof)**: Deep case study with metrics, timeline, and implementation ease. Address the "how hard is this to implement" objection. CTA: "Can I send you the implementation timeline?"
- **Email 5 (Breakup)**: Acknowledge timing may be off. Offer a resource that is valuable regardless of purchase. CTA: "Happy to reconnect when timing is right."

## Cadence Science

### Optimal Send Timing

| Day of Week | Best Time (Prospect's Timezone) | Rationale |
|-------------|--------------------------------|-----------|
| Tuesday | 8:00-9:00 AM | Inbox is processed but not yet overwhelmed |
| Wednesday | 8:00-9:00 AM | Mid-week productivity peak |
| Thursday | 7:30-8:30 AM | Slightly earlier to beat the Thursday email surge |

**Avoid**: Monday morning (inbox backlog), Friday afternoon (weekend mode), weekends (signals desperation and gets buried).

### Follow-Up Intervals

| Email | Standard Cadence | C-Suite Cadence | IC Cadence |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|------------|
| Email 1 → Email 2 | 4 days | 7 days | 3 days |
| Email 2 → Email 3 | 6 days | 10 days | 4 days |
| Email 3 → Email 4 | 8 days | 12 days | 6 days |
| Email 4 → Email 5 | 5 days | 8 days | 4 days |
| **Total sequence** | **23 days** | **37 days** | **17 days** |

**Principle**: Higher seniority = wider spacing. Executives have less inbox time and more gatekeepers. Compressed cadences for executives signal desperation.

### Channel Sequencing

| Touchpoint | Channel | Purpose |
|------------|---------|---------|
| Touch 1 | Email | Primary — establishes the conversation |
| Touch 2 | LinkedIn connect | Secondary — creates visibility and social proof |
| Touch 3 | Email | Reinforces the message from a different angle |
| Touch 4 | LinkedIn message | Adds a personal, informal channel |
| Touch 5 | Email | Deepens the value proposition |
| Touch 6 | Phone call | High-signal — demonstrates commitment |
| Touch 7 | Email | Case study / social proof |
| Touch 8 | Email (breakup) | Final touch with value offer |

**Multi-channel principle**: Never send the same message on two channels. Each channel carries a different version of the value proposition. Email = detailed, LinkedIn = conversational, Phone = direct ask.

## Personalization Hierarchy

Personalization tokens are ranked by impact and effort. Use the highest-impact tokens available; do not default to low-impact tokens when better data exists.

### Tier 1: Trigger Event (Highest Impact)
- Recent funding round, acquisition, or IPO
- New executive hire in the relevant function
- Product launch or major announcement
- Regulatory change affecting their industry
- Competitor mentioned in press or earnings call

**Why highest impact**: Trigger events create urgency and demonstrate you are paying attention right now, not sending a batch email.

### Tier 2: Company Context
- Company growth trajectory (hiring patterns, revenue milestones)
- Industry-specific challenges (regulatory, competitive, technological)
- Tech stack signals (job postings mentioning specific tools)
- Recent press coverage or analyst mentions

**Why high impact**: Company context shows you have done homework beyond reading their LinkedIn headline.

### Tier 3: Role Context
- Common pain points for their specific title and seniority
- KPIs they are likely measured on
- Tools they likely use daily
- Peers they likely interact with

**Why moderate impact**: Role-based personalization is better than nothing but feels templated if not combined with Tier 1 or 2.

### Tier 4: Individual Context
- Mutual connections (use sparingly — feels manufactured if forced)
- Content they have published or shared
- Conference appearances or speaking engagements
- Career trajectory (promotions, company changes)

**Why moderate impact**: Individual details demonstrate genuine interest but must feel organic, not stalker-ish.

### Tier 5: Generic (Lowest Impact)
- Company name and prospect first name
- Industry vertical
- Company size bucket

**Why lowest impact**: Every automated email uses these. They are table stakes, not differentiation.

## Subject Line Best Practices

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Keep under 6 words | Write full sentences |
| Use lowercase (feels personal) | Use ALL CAPS or excessive punctuation |
| Ask a question | Make a claim about your product |
| Reference their company or role | Use "I" or "we" as the first word |
| Test genuinely different approaches in A/B | Change one word between variants |

**High-performing subject line patterns**:
- `{company} + {pain point}?` — e.g., "Acme's onboarding bottleneck?"
- `{mutual connection} suggested I reach out` — only if true
- `{trigger event} → question` — e.g., "post-Series B hiring plans?"
- `{metric} for {role}` — e.g., "2x reply rate for SDR teams"
- `quick question, {first name}` — simple, curiosity-driven

## Email Body Structure

Every cold email should follow this structure:

1. **Opening hook** (1 sentence): Demonstrate relevance immediately. Reference a trigger event, pain point, or shared context. Never open with "I am..." or "We are..."
2. **Problem/value bridge** (2-3 sentences): Connect the hook to the prospect's pain and your value proposition. Use the selected framework (AIDA/PAS/BAB) to structure this section.
3. **Proof point** (1 sentence): One specific metric, customer name, or result. Not a paragraph — one line.
4. **CTA** (1 sentence): One specific, low-friction ask. Not "let me know your thoughts" — a concrete next step with a time commitment.

**Total length**: 50-125 words. Under 50 feels incomplete. Over 125 does not get read.
