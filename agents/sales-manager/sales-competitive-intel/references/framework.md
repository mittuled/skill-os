# Framework: Sales Competitive Intel

This framework defines the 8-dimension competitive analysis structure, battle card format, switching cost categories, and win/loss analysis methodology.

## 8-Dimension Competitive Analysis

### 1. Product Features
- Core capabilities and feature set
- Platform architecture (monolith, modular, API-first)
- Integration ecosystem (native integrations, marketplace, API coverage)
- Product velocity (release cadence, recent launches)
- Guidance: focus on features relevant to your ICP's requirements, not exhaustive feature lists

### 2. Pricing Model
- Pricing structure (per-seat, usage-based, flat-rate, hybrid)
- Published pricing vs. negotiated pricing (if known)
- Contract terms (annual, multi-year, month-to-month)
- Hidden costs (implementation, training, premium support, overage charges)
- Guidance: compare total cost of ownership, not sticker price

### 3. Market Position
- Target market segment (SMB, mid-market, enterprise)
- Market share estimate (if available)
- Analyst positioning (Gartner, Forrester, G2 category rank)
- Brand perception and narrative
- Guidance: note where their positioning overlaps with yours vs. where they target different segments

### 4. Strengths (Where They Win)
- Specific capabilities where they have a genuine advantage
- Customer segments where they consistently beat you
- Deal characteristics that favor them (e.g., specific industry, company size, use case)
- Guidance: be honest — AEs need to know this to prepare, not to be demoralized

### 5. Weaknesses (Where We Win)
- Specific gaps or limitations relative to your solution
- Customer complaints and pain points (from review sites, churn data)
- Deal characteristics that favor you
- Guidance: cite evidence — review quotes, feature gaps, support complaints — not opinion

### 6. Customer Sentiment
- G2, Capterra, TrustRadius ratings and review themes
- Case studies and published customer stories
- Known customer churn and reasons (if available)
- NPS or satisfaction data (if public)
- Guidance: extract specific quotes that AEs can reference in conversations

### 7. Recent Moves
- Funding rounds, M&A activity, IPO signals
- Product launches and roadmap announcements
- Key hires and departures (especially sales and product leadership)
- Partnership announcements
- Guidance: flag moves that change the competitive dynamic, not routine press releases

### 8. Differentiation Strategy
- Their stated positioning and messaging
- How they differentiate against you specifically (if known)
- Their ideal customer narrative
- Guidance: understanding how they position against you reveals their perceived weaknesses in your offering

## Battle Card Structure

Each battle card follows a 7-section format designed for quick reference during live calls:

### Section 1: Competitor Overview
- One-paragraph summary: who they are, who they target, what they do well
- Key stats: founded, funding, employee count, estimated customers
- Format: scannable in 15 seconds

### Section 2: Strengths (Where They Win)
- 3-5 bullet points of genuine advantages
- Each bullet includes evidence (customer quote, feature, metric)
- Format: honest assessment — this section builds AE credibility when they acknowledge competitor strengths to prospects

### Section 3: Weaknesses (Where We Win)
- 3-5 bullet points of genuine gaps or limitations
- Each bullet includes evidence and the business impact on the prospect
- Format: focus on gaps that matter to your ICP, not obscure missing features

### Section 4: Common Objections with Responses
- Top 5 objections prospects raise after talking to this competitor
- Per objection: the objection, the response (conversational tone), and the proof point
- Format: written as dialogue AEs can use verbatim in calls

### Section 5: Trap Questions They Ask
- Questions the competitor's sales team coaches prospects to ask you
- Why it is a trap (what it is designed to expose)
- How to respond honestly without falling into the trap
- Format: question, trap explanation, recommended response

### Section 6: Talk Tracks
- 3-5 talk tracks for common scenarios:
  - "The prospect just mentioned they are also looking at [Competitor]"
  - "The prospect says [Competitor] is cheaper"
  - "The prospect says [Competitor] has feature X that you do not"
  - "The prospect is currently using [Competitor] and considering switching"
- Format: conversational, first-person, ready to use in live conversation

### Section 7: Proof Points
- 3-5 customer stories or data points that directly counter this competitor
- Each proof point includes: customer name (if public) or anonymized profile, the competitive context, and the quantified result
- Format: one paragraph each, optimized for verbal delivery

## Switching Cost Categories

### 1. Financial Costs
- Remaining contract value with current vendor (buyout cost)
- Overlap period costs (running both systems during migration)
- New implementation and onboarding costs
- Training costs for the new system
- Calculation: sum all financial costs to determine the "switching tax"

### 2. Operational Costs
- Process redesign effort (workflows built around current tool)
- Team retraining time and productivity dip during transition
- Change management overhead (communication, adoption monitoring)
- Estimate: hours × loaded labor rate for affected team members

### 3. Data Costs
- Data migration complexity (format compatibility, field mapping)
- Historical data preservation (can it transfer, or is it lost)
- Integration reconfiguration (downstream systems connected to current tool)
- Risk: data loss or corruption during migration

### 4. Relationship Costs
- Champion risk (the internal champion may have advocated for the current vendor)
- Vendor relationship value (existing trust, negotiated terms, institutional knowledge)
- Stakeholder fatigue (evaluation and implementation burden on the team)
- Risk: champion becomes a blocker if they feel their judgment is questioned

### 5. Opportunity Costs
- Implementation downtime (period between old system off and new system productive)
- Delayed value realization (time to reach current system's utility level)
- Resource diversion (team focused on migration instead of core work)
- Calculation: monthly value of current solution × months of transition

## Win/Loss Analysis Methodology

### Data Collection
- CRM disposition codes for every closed-won and closed-lost with competitor mentioned
- Win/loss interviews (at minimum, capture: why they chose us / why they did not, which competitors they evaluated, what almost made them choose the alternative)
- Deal stage analysis: at which stage did we win or lose the deal

### Analysis Dimensions
1. **Win rate by competitor**: overall and segmented by deal size, industry, and company size
2. **Deal velocity by competitor**: average days in pipeline when competitor is present vs. absent
3. **Stage-specific loss rate**: at which stage do deals against this competitor most often die
4. **Objection correlation**: which objections appear most frequently in lost deals vs. won deals
5. **Proof point correlation**: which proof points or case studies appear most frequently in won deals
6. **Price sensitivity**: how often is pricing cited as the reason for loss vs. other factors

### Output Format
Per competitor, produce:
- Win rate trend (trailing 4 quarters)
- Top 3 reasons we win against them (with evidence count)
- Top 3 reasons we lose to them (with evidence count)
- The single highest-leverage improvement that would increase win rate
