# Research Methodology Guide: Customer Discovery

## Purpose

Selection criteria and practical guidance for choosing and applying discovery research methods to answer product and design questions.

## Method Selection Decision Table

| Research Question Type | Recommended Method | Why |
|-----------------------|-------------------|-----|
| What problems do users have? | Semi-structured interviews | Open-ended exploration; uncovers unarticulated needs |
| How do users currently solve a problem? | Contextual inquiry / diary study | Observes real behaviour in natural context |
| What do users think about a concept? | Concept testing | Direct reaction to design artefacts |
| How many users have problem X? | Survey (quantitative) | Scale validation of qualitative findings |
| Where do users drop off in a flow? | Funnel analytics + session recordings | Quantitative signal with qualitative context |
| What do users prioritise? | Card sorting / Kano model survey | Structured prioritisation data |

## Research Design Checklist

Before starting any discovery study:

- [ ] Research question is single and specific (not compound)
- [ ] Participant criteria define segment precisely (not "our users")
- [ ] Sample size justified (see table below)
- [ ] Methodology matches question type (table above)
- [ ] Discussion guide reviewed by at least one team member
- [ ] Screener survey written and tested
- [ ] Consent form prepared (recording, data use)
- [ ] Incentive confirmed (amount, delivery method)
- [ ] Analysis method documented before data collection begins

## Sample Size Guidelines

| Study Type | Minimum | Ideal | Rationale |
|-----------|---------|-------|-----------|
| Exploratory interviews | 5 | 8-12 | Diminishing returns after 8 for homogeneous segment |
| Contextual inquiry | 3 | 5-8 | Observation is time-intensive; 5 reveals key patterns |
| Usability testing | 5 | 8 | Nielsen: 5 users catch 85% of issues per segment |
| Concept testing | 5 | 8-10 | Balance speed with signal quality |
| Quantitative survey | 100 | 300+ | Statistical significance for ±5% margin of error |

Stratify samples across user segments if testing multiple personas — do not pool.

## Discussion Guide Structure

| Section | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Warm-up | 5 min | Build rapport; confirm participant background |
| Contextual background | 10 min | Understand current behaviour and workflow without leading |
| Problem exploration | 20 min | Explore pain points, frustrations, and goals |
| Deep dive | 10 min | Follow specific threads surfaced in exploration |
| Concept reaction (if applicable) | 10 min | Show stimulus and capture reactions |
| Wrap-up | 5 min | Open "anything else?"; thank participant |

**Total**: 55-60 min for in-depth interview

## Participant Recruitment Criteria Template

Define participants using five dimensions:

| Dimension | Definition | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Role / job | Job function or life stage | "B2B: operations manager in a company 50-500 employees" |
| Behaviour | Specific behaviour that qualifies | "Has managed a project with 3+ stakeholders in the last 3 months" |
| Experience | Product or category experience | "Has used at least one project management tool in the past year" |
| Exclusions | Who to screen out | "Exclude employees of direct competitors" |
| Diversity | Demographic and context variety | "Minimum 40% non-male; at least 2 participants from outside US" |

## Qualitative Bias Mitigation

| Bias | Description | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Leading questions | Phrasing that suggests the desired answer | Ask "How do you currently...?" not "Is it frustrating when...?" |
| Confirmation bias | Noticing only evidence that confirms hypothesis | Write findings before discussing with team; use independent coders |
| Social desirability | Participants give "nice" answers to please researcher | Observe behaviour over self-report; use indirect questions |
| Sample bias | Recruiting participants who are easy to reach | Use screeners to enforce criteria; avoid internal staff and power users only |
| Recency bias | Most memorable event dominates recall | Ask about specific recent instances ("Tell me about the last time...") |

## Ethical Standards

- [ ] Participants are never deceived about the purpose of the study
- [ ] Informed consent obtained before recording begins
- [ ] Participants can withdraw at any time without penalty
- [ ] Personally identifiable information (PII) stored separately from session data
- [ ] Incentives do not coerce participation from vulnerable populations
- [ ] Study protocols reviewed by a senior researcher before field work begins
