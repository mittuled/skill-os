# Interview Techniques Reference

## Purpose

Practical techniques for conducting high-quality user research interviews that surface genuine user behaviour, motivations, and needs.

## Question Type Taxonomy

| Type | Purpose | Example | Use When |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| Open | Explore territory freely | "Tell me about how you manage your projects." | Warm-up; topic exploration |
| Probing | Dig deeper into a response | "You mentioned it was frustrating — can you say more about that?" | After any response worth exploring |
| Clarifying | Ensure accurate understanding | "When you say 'the process breaks down' — what specifically happens?" | When a response is vague |
| Redirecting | Return to key topic without leading | "That's interesting — I want to come back to the moment you first noticed the problem..." | When off-topic |
| Hypothetical | Surface implicit preferences | "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about this, what would it be?" | Late in interview to surface aspiration |
| Laddering | Surface deeper motivations (why behind why) | "Why is that important to you?" (asked 2-3 times) | When finding functional jobs; need emotional layer |

## The Laddering Technique

Ask "why" iteratively to move from task to goal to value:

```
Level 1 (Attribute): "I use a spreadsheet for tracking."
Question: "Why a spreadsheet specifically?"

Level 2 (Functional benefit): "Because I can customise the columns."
Question: "Why does being able to customise columns matter?"

Level 3 (Emotional benefit): "Because then I see exactly what I need without noise."
Question: "Why is seeing exactly what you need important?"

Level 4 (Value): "Because I'm responsible for this team and I can't afford to miss anything."
```

The Level 4 answer is the job. Stop laddering when the participant gives an intrinsic values statement.

## Active Listening Signals

| Technique | When to Use | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Affirmative mirroring | Encourage elaboration | Nod; "Mm-hmm"; "Go on." |
| Silence | Allow processing and continuation | Pause 3-5 seconds after a key answer before asking the next question |
| Paraphrasing | Confirm understanding | "So if I understand correctly, the main issue is..." |
| Emotional labelling | Validate and deepen emotional content | "It sounds like that was really frustrating." |
| Anchor on specific events | Prevent hypothetical and generic responses | "Let's go back to that specific project — what happened next?" |

## Bias Control Techniques

| Bias Risk | Technique | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Confirmation bias | Actively seek disconfirming evidence | "Can you tell me about a time when [approach you're testing] didn't work?" |
| Leading questions | Use neutral framing | "What happened next?" not "Was it confusing?" |
| Hypothetical answers | Anchor to specific past events | "Tell me about the last time you actually did that" |
| Social desirability | Normalise the "wrong" answer | "Some people find this easy, others find it hard — what was your experience?" |
| Acquiescence bias | Ask for negative experiences explicitly | "What would you change about this?" before "What works well?" |

## Think-Aloud Protocol

For usability tests and design feedback sessions:

1. Demonstrate think-aloud: "As you do this task, say whatever comes to mind — what you're looking at, what you're thinking, what's confusing."
2. Practice with a warm-up task: "Think aloud while you find the volume control on this device."
3. Prompt if silent for >10 seconds: "What are you thinking right now?"
4. Do not interpret — describe: "You paused on that screen — can you tell me what was going through your mind?"

## Interview Recording and Note-Taking

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Video recording (with consent) | Full session available for review; can share clips | Requires consent; may inhibit some participants |
| Audio recording + notes | Less intrusive; good coverage | Misses non-verbal behaviour |
| Dual-facilitator model (one leads, one notes) | Full facilitator attention on participant; rich notes | Requires two researchers |
| Live notes only | Minimal setup | Gaps in coverage; influences memory during analysis |

**Recommended**: Video recording + separate note-taker when budget allows. Solo researcher: audio record + brief notes during, detailed notes within 30 min post-session.

## Post-Session Debrief Protocol (within 30 minutes)

Capture:
1. Most important insight from this session (1 sentence)
2. Most surprising moment
3. Strongest quote (verbatim)
4. Hypotheses confirmed or challenged
5. Patterns connecting to previous sessions

Write before discussing with co-researcher — prevents anchoring on colleague's interpretation.

## Red Flags: When Interview Quality Is Compromised

| Signal | Risk | Mitigation |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Participant gives "yes/no" answers consistently | Not elaborating; low engagement | Use open questions more; invite with "Tell me more..." |
| Participant is very agreeable to everything shown | Social desirability; no critical feedback surfacing | Explicitly invite criticism: "What would you remove?" |
| Participant speaks hypothetically throughout | Responses reflect preference, not behaviour | Redirect to "Tell me about the last time you actually did that" |
| Session runs short (under 30 min for 60-min study) | Insufficient depth | Probe more; use follow-up email questions if needed |
| Participant doesn't match screener | Data is contaminated | Note in session record; exclude from primary analysis if material mismatch |
