# Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework Reference

## Origin and Core Concept

Jobs-to-be-Done, developed by Clayton Christensen and refined by Bob Moesta, frames user motivation as "hiring" a product to accomplish a job. Users do not buy products — they hire them to make progress in their lives.

**Core insight**: Features do not drive adoption — the progress users are trying to make does. Understanding the job precedes designing the solution.

## The Three Job Dimensions

Every job has three layers:

| Dimension | Definition | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Functional job | The practical task to be accomplished | "Send a file to a colleague" |
| Emotional job | How the user wants to feel during or after | "Feel in control and professional" |
| Social job | How the user wants to be perceived by others | "Be seen as organised and responsive" |

**Design implication**: A product that only solves the functional job while ignoring emotional and social dimensions loses to a product that does all three.

## Job Statement Structure

A valid JTBD statement has three parts:

```
When [situation / trigger],
I want to [motivation / job],
So I can [expected outcome].
```

**Example**:
> "When I finish a client meeting, I want to quickly capture what was agreed and who is responsible, so I can keep my reputation for following through on commitments."

### Validation Criteria for Job Statements

| Criterion | Check |
|-----------|-------|
| Situation is specific | Describes a real, triggering moment — not a general state |
| Motivation is the job, not the solution | "capture what was agreed" not "use a note-taking tool" |
| Outcome is the progress, not the feature | "keep my reputation" not "see a task list" |
| Applies to multiple solutions | The job statement should be solution-agnostic |

## Forces of Progress Model (Switch Interview)

The four forces that determine whether a user adopts a new solution:

| Force | Direction | Definition |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| Push (problem with current situation) | Toward change | Frustration with current solution drives motivation to switch |
| Pull (attraction of new solution) | Toward change | Perceived improvement the new product offers |
| Anxiety (uncertainty about new solution) | Against change | Fear that the new product will be hard to use or won't work |
| Habit (attachment to current solution) | Against change | Familiarity and established routine resist switching |

**Design application**: Reduce anxiety (clear onboarding, social proof, reversible actions) while increasing pull (outcome clarity, quick wins).

## JTBD Interview Questions

Questions to surface the job in a research interview:

### Trigger and Context
- "Tell me about the moment you first started looking for a solution to [topic]."
- "What happened that made [old approach] no longer good enough?"
- "Walk me through the day you decided to [start using / switch to] [product category]."

### Job and Motivation
- "What were you hoping to be able to do that you couldn't before?"
- "What does success look like after you've done that job well?"
- "What would it cost you — professionally or personally — if you couldn't do that?"

### Forces
- "What almost stopped you from making the switch?"
- "What gave you confidence to go ahead anyway?"
- "Is there a part of [old solution] you miss or wish the new solution had?"

## JTBD Mapping Taxonomy

| Job Type | Definition | Frequency | Value |
|----------|------------|-----------|-------|
| Core job | The primary job the product is hired for | High | High |
| Related job | Adjacent job that users want to do while doing the core job | Medium | Medium |
| Emotional / social job | How users want to feel or be perceived | Varies | Often underweighted |
| Consumption job | The job of acquiring, setting up, and learning the product | One-time | High for adoption |
| Maintenance job | Keeping the product working and current over time | Recurring | Critical for retention |

## Common JTBD Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|-------------|-------------|
| Describing the job in terms of the product feature | Locks thinking to the current solution; misses true motivation |
| Conflating the job with the task | Tasks are steps; jobs are outcomes. "Fill in the form" is a task; "Get the permit approved" is the job |
| Ignoring non-consumption | Some users are not switching from a competitor — they are switching from doing nothing |
| Treating all users as having the same job | Different user segments often hire the same product for different jobs |
| Skipping the situation trigger | Jobs without a specific triggering situation are too abstract to design against |
