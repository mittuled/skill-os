# Negotiation Framework: Contract Negotiation Theory

This framework provides the theoretical foundation for building data-driven contract negotiation strategies.

## Core Concepts

### BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)

The fallback option if negotiations fail. A strong BATNA increases leverage; a weak BATNA increases pressure to close. Always quantify:
- **Your BATNA**: What is the concrete alternative? What does it cost in time, money, and opportunity?
- **Their BATNA**: What happens to the counterparty if this deal fails? Are they under time pressure, revenue pressure, or competitive pressure?

### WATNA (Worst Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)

The worst-case outcome if no deal is reached. Useful for calibrating walk-away thresholds — if the WATNA is catastrophic, the negotiation team may accept less favorable terms than the BATNA alone would suggest.

### ZOPA (Zone Of Possible Agreement)

The overlap between your minimum acceptable outcome and the counterparty's maximum offer. If ZOPA exists, a deal is possible. If not, one or both parties must adjust expectations or the deal will fail. Map ZOPA term by term, not just on price.

## Concession Planning

### Reciprocity Principle

Every concession should be met with a concession. Never concede unilaterally — always frame concessions as trades: "We can move on X if you can move on Y."

### Diminishing Concessions

Each successive concession should be smaller than the last. This signals that you are approaching your limit. Pattern: if your first concession is 100 units, the second should be ~50, the third ~25. If the counterparty sees constant-size concessions, they will expect more.

### Package Deals vs. Item-by-Item

- **Package deals**: Bundle multiple terms into a single proposal. Preferred when terms are interrelated (e.g., price + payment terms + liability cap). Allows creative trades across dimensions.
- **Item-by-item**: Negotiate each term independently. Simpler but limits trade opportunities and can create deadlocks on individual terms.

Prefer package deals for complex contracts. Use item-by-item only for simple agreements with few variables.

## Counterparty Analysis

### Key Questions

1. **Market position**: Are they a dominant player or competing for your business?
2. **Deal urgency**: What is their timeline? Quarter-end pressure? Board deadlines?
3. **Alternative options**: Who else could they work with? How costly is switching?
4. **Regulatory obligations**: Are they constrained by compliance requirements that limit their flexibility on certain terms?
5. **Precedent sensitivity**: Will this deal set precedent for their other customers/vendors?
6. **Decision authority**: Is the negotiator empowered to agree, or must they escalate?

### Inferring Priorities

The counterparty's priorities can be inferred from:
- Which terms they redline first (high priority)
- Which terms they accept without comment (low priority)
- Their initial markup pattern (aggressive on price = price-sensitive; aggressive on liability = risk-averse)
- Public information (earnings calls, press releases, regulatory filings)

## Opening Position Strategy

### Anchor-and-Adjust Technique

Set your opening position at the optimistic end of the defensible range. Research shows the first number on the table disproportionately influences the final outcome. Rules:
- The anchor must be justifiable — cite market data, benchmarks, or precedent
- Never anchor so aggressively that it damages credibility
- Prepare a rationale for your anchor before presenting it

## Term Prioritization Matrix

| Tier | Definition | Negotiation Behavior |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Must-have** | Non-negotiable; walk-away triggers if not achieved | Defend aggressively, escalate if needed, never concede without equivalent must-have from counterparty |
| **Nice-to-have** | Meaningful value but survivable without | Pursue firmly, concede only in exchange for must-have terms or multiple nice-to-haves |
| **Tradeable** | Low business impact; useful as bargaining chips | Concede strategically to build goodwill or extract value on higher-tier terms |

## Escalation Framework

| Signal | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Counterparty rejects all must-have terms | Escalate to senior leadership for BATNA reassessment |
| Negotiations stall for >2 rounds on same term | Propose package deal or creative restructuring |
| Counterparty introduces new non-standard terms | Pause to assess risk before responding |
| Walk-away threshold approached | Final offer with clear deadline |
| Counterparty signals willingness to walk | Reassess BATNA urgency; consider strategic concession |
