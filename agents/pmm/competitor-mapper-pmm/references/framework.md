# Framework: Competitive Landscape Mapping

Reference framework for structuring the competitive mapping process, based on established competitive intelligence methodologies.

## Core Model: Three-Tier Competitor Classification

Competitors are classified by deal presence, not company size:

| Tier | Definition | Analysis Depth | Update Cadence |
|------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Primary | Appears in >10% of competitive deals | Full profile: positioning, pricing, features, roadmap, funding, wins/losses | Monthly |
| Secondary | Appears occasionally in deals or analyst reports | Standard profile: positioning, pricing, key differentiators | Quarterly |
| Emerging | Early-stage, adjacent, or newly funded entrants | Watch profile: overview, funding, positioning claim | Quarterly scan |

## Competitive Matrix Dimensions

Score each competitor 1-5 on these evaluation dimensions (adapt to your market):

| Dimension | What It Measures | Data Sources |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| Positioning Clarity | How clearly the competitor communicates what they do and for whom | Website, analyst reports, demo recordings |
| Pricing Competitiveness | Price-to-value ratio relative to market expectations | Published pricing, sales intel, G2 reviews |
| Feature Breadth | Coverage of core use cases for the shared ICP | Product documentation, G2 feature grid, trial accounts |
| Integration Ecosystem | Depth and quality of integrations with adjacent tools | Marketplace listings, partnership announcements |
| Market Perception | Analyst positioning, review-site ratings, social sentiment | Gartner/Forrester, G2/TrustRadius scores, social listening |
| Customer Base Strength | Size, logo quality, and segment concentration of customer base | Case studies, press releases, annual reports |

## Positioning Map Construction (April Dunford Method)

Based on April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" framework:

1. **Identify competitive alternatives**: What would the buyer do if your product did not exist?
2. **List unique attributes**: Capabilities only your product has (or does significantly better)
3. **Map attributes to value**: Translate unique attributes into buyer-meaningful value themes
4. **Select map axes**: Choose the two value themes that most differentiate your product from the competitive field
5. **Plot competitors**: Place each competitor on the map based on objective evidence, not aspiration

### Axis Selection Criteria
- Axes must represent dimensions buyers care about (validated by win/loss or customer interviews)
- Axes must create separation (if all competitors cluster, the axes are wrong)
- Common axis pairs: ease-of-use vs. enterprise depth, breadth vs. specialisation, speed-to-value vs. customisation depth

## White Space Analysis

After plotting the positioning map, identify white space using:

| Quadrant | Competitor Density | Your Product | Recommendation |
|----------|-------------------|--------------|----------------|
| High density, you present | Crowded | Competing | Sharpen differentiation messaging |
| High density, you absent | Crowded | Not competing | Evaluate if the segment is worth entering |
| Low density, you present | Open | Positioned | Emphasise ownership of this space |
| Low density, you absent | Open | Not positioned | Assess alignment with roadmap and ICP |

## Data Collection Checklist Per Competitor

- [ ] Company overview (founded, HQ, employee count, funding)
- [ ] Target ICP and buyer persona
- [ ] Core positioning statement (from website hero section)
- [ ] Pricing model and published tiers
- [ ] Top 5 differentiators (from their perspective)
- [ ] Known weaknesses (from reviews, win/loss, sales intel)
- [ ] Recent funding, M&A, or partnership activity
- [ ] Product release velocity (changelog frequency)
- [ ] Key customer logos and case studies
- [ ] Analyst coverage and placement
