# Framework: Product Positioning (April Dunford Method)

Reference framework for crafting product positioning, based on April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" positioning methodology.

## The Five Components of Positioning

Positioning is not a tagline. It is the strategic context that makes your product's value obvious to the right buyer.

| Component | Question It Answers | Example |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| Competitive Alternatives | What would the buyer do if you did not exist? | Spreadsheets + manual processes, Competitor X, hiring a consultant |
| Unique Attributes | What can you do that alternatives cannot? | Real-time collaboration on pricing models, API-first architecture |
| Value | What does the unique attribute enable for the buyer? | Close deals 40% faster, eliminate data re-entry across systems |
| Target Buyer | Who cares most about that value? | Mid-market SaaS companies with 50-500 employees, RevOps teams |
| Market Category | What market do you position yourself in? | Revenue intelligence platform, sales enablement software |

## Positioning Statement Template

> For **[target buyer]** who **[key need/pain]**, **[product]** is a **[market category]** that **[primary benefit]**. Unlike **[competitive alternative]**, **[product]** **[key differentiator]**.

## Messaging Hierarchy Structure

The positioning statement generates a messaging hierarchy:

```
Positioning Statement (internal strategic document)
├── Headline (12 words max, benefit-oriented)
├── Sub-headline (expands with specificity)
├── Pillar 1: [Claim + Evidence + Buyer Outcome]
├── Pillar 2: [Claim + Evidence + Buyer Outcome]
├── Pillar 3: [Claim + Evidence + Buyer Outcome]
└── CTA (clear next action for the buyer)
```

### Pillar Construction Rules
- Each pillar maps to one unique attribute from the positioning canvas
- Evidence must be external (customer quote, metric, third-party validation), not internal assertion
- Buyer outcome must answer "so what?" from the buyer's perspective

## Positioning Stress Test

Every positioning statement must pass three tests:

| Test | Question | Failure Mode |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Truth | Can you prove this claim with evidence? | Aspirational positioning that sales cannot defend |
| Relevance | Does the target buyer actually care about this? | Technically accurate but irrelevant to purchase decisions |
| Uniqueness | Does any competitor credibly claim the same thing? | Generic positioning that fails to differentiate |

## Competitive Positioning Stances

| Stance | When to Use | Risk |
|--------|------------|------|
| Leader | You have the strongest product and market share | Complacency; ignoring emerging challengers |
| Challenger | You are strong but not the market leader | Over-focusing on the leader instead of your own story |
| Niche | You own a specific segment better than anyone | Being perceived as limited when trying to expand |
| Disruptor | You are redefining the category or creating a new one | Buyers do not understand the category; sales cycles lengthen |

## Positioning Review Cadence

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Semi-annual (scheduled) | Full positioning review against current market data |
| Major competitor repositioning | Assess whether differentiation claims still hold |
| Product launch (Tier 1) | Evaluate whether new capabilities strengthen or shift positioning |
| Win-rate decline (>5pp drop) | Investigate whether positioning resonance has degraded |
| New market segment entry | Create segment-specific positioning variant |

## Common Positioning Pitfalls

1. **Feature-as-positioning**: "We have feature X" is not positioning; "Feature X enables outcome Y for buyer Z" is
2. **Positioning by aspiration**: Claiming what you plan to be, not what you are today
3. **Category confusion**: Positioning in a category buyers do not recognise forces you to sell the category before selling the product
4. **Differentiation decay**: A differentiator today is table stakes tomorrow; positioning requires ongoing maintenance
