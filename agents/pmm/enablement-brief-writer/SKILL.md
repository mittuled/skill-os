# enablement-brief-writer

## Agent: PMM
L2 product marketing manager responsible for competitive intelligence, positioning, GTM planning, pricing strategy, launch narrative, and sales enablement.

Department ethos: [ideal-product.md](../../../departments/product/ideal-product.md)

## Skill Description
Writes the sales and CS enablement brief for each product launch, translating product capabilities and positioning into field-ready talk tracks, objection handlers, and discovery questions.

## When to Use
- A new feature or product is approaching launch and field teams need to sell or support it.
- Pricing or packaging changes require updated selling motions.
- Positioning has been refreshed and existing talk tracks are outdated.
- A competitive response demands new objection-handling guidance.
- CS needs onboarding or expansion talking points for an existing-customer rollout.

## Workflow
1. Gather inputs: positioning document, launch narrative, pricing details, competitive context, and target persona/ICP definition.
2. Identify the audience for the brief. Separate sections for AEs, SDRs, SEs, and CS if their needs diverge.
3. Write the value proposition summary in three tiers: one-liner, elevator pitch (30 seconds), and full narrative (two minutes).
4. Draft discovery questions that surface the pain points the new capability solves.
5. Build objection-handling blocks. For each anticipated objection, provide the objection statement, the reframe, a proof point, and a bridge back to value.
6. Include competitive positioning guidance: what to say when a specific competitor is mentioned, and what not to say.
7. Add a quick-reference pricing or packaging summary if relevant to the launch.
8. Review the brief with sales leadership and one or two senior AEs for field accuracy. Incorporate feedback.
9. Publish the brief to the enablement platform and announce distribution through the standard enablement channel.

## Anti-Patterns
- **Writing the brief after launch day.** *Why: Field teams improvise messaging during the critical first week, and inconsistent narratives are hard to correct once they take hold.*
- **Using product-centric language instead of buyer-centric language.** *Why: Reps need to articulate customer outcomes, not internal feature names, in conversations with buyers.*
- **Skipping objection handlers.** *Why: The brief's most-used section in live deals is objection handling; without it, reps either freeze or freelance.*
- **Creating a 20-page document no one reads.** *Why: Enablement briefs must be scannable in under five minutes; lengthy documents get bookmarked and ignored.*

## Output

**Success:**
- A concise enablement brief (two to four pages) with value prop tiers, discovery questions, objection handlers, and competitive guidance.
- Published to the enablement platform before launch day.
- Positive feedback from at least one sales leader confirming field relevance.

**Failure:**
- Brief delivered after reps have already started selling the new capability with improvised messaging.
- Content that reads like a product spec rather than a selling guide.
- No objection-handling section, leaving reps unprepared for predictable buyer pushback.
