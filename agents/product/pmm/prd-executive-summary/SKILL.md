# prd-executive-summary

## Agent: PMM
L2 product marketing manager responsible for competitive intelligence, positioning, GTM planning, pricing strategy, launch narrative, and sales enablement.

Department ethos: [ideal-product.md](../../../../departments/product/ideal-product.md)

## Skill Description
Writes the executive summary of the PRD for leadership and cross-functional stakeholders, translating the product specification into a concise, market-oriented framing that explains what is being built, why it matters to buyers, and how it fits the strategic narrative.

## When to Use
- A PRD is nearing final review and needs an executive summary before leadership sign-off.
- A cross-functional audience (sales, CS, marketing, finance) needs to understand a product initiative without reading the full spec.
- Board or investor materials require a one-page summary of a major product bet.
- Multiple PRDs are in flight and leadership needs a standardised format to compare and prioritise.

## Workflow
1. Read the full PRD. Identify the problem statement, proposed solution, target user, success metrics, and key risks.
2. Extract the market context. Why does this initiative matter now? Reference competitive pressure, customer demand signals, or market trend data.
3. Write the executive summary in four sections: Market Context (two to three sentences on why this matters), Solution Overview (what the product will do in buyer-centric language), Expected Impact (metrics and outcomes), and Key Risks (top two to three risks with mitigations).
4. Keep the summary to one page or under 500 words. Executives scan, not read.
5. Use buyer language, not engineering language. Replace internal jargon with the terms customers and prospects use.
6. Review with the PM who owns the PRD to confirm accuracy and alignment with the full spec.
7. Review with marketing leadership to confirm the summary aligns with current positioning and narrative.
8. Insert the executive summary as the opening section of the PRD and distribute to the leadership review group.

## Anti-Patterns
- **Summarising features instead of framing the market opportunity.** *Why: Leadership approves initiatives based on strategic fit and market impact, not feature lists.*
- **Writing the summary before reading the full PRD.** *Why: Superficial summaries that miss key constraints or risks undermine PMM credibility and lead to misaligned expectations.*
- **Using engineering terminology.** *Why: The audience is cross-functional; technical jargon excludes sales, CS, and finance stakeholders from the conversation.*
- **Exceeding one page.** *Why: An executive summary that requires more than five minutes to read defeats its purpose; brevity is a feature, not a limitation.*

## Output

**Success:**
- A one-page executive summary with market context, solution overview, expected impact, and key risks.
- Confirmed accurate by the PM and aligned with positioning by marketing leadership.
- Inserted as the opening section of the PRD before the leadership review cycle.

**Failure:**
- A summary that reads like a condensed spec rather than a strategic framing.
- Summary published without PM review, containing inaccuracies that erode cross-functional trust.
- Summary longer than one page, reducing the likelihood that leadership reads it.
