# pricing-finaliser-pmm

## Agent: PMM
L2 product marketing manager responsible for competitive intelligence, positioning, GTM planning, pricing strategy, launch narrative, and sales enablement.

Department ethos: [ideal-product.md](../../../../departments/product/ideal-product.md)

## Skill Description
Finalises pricing tiers and packaging for product launch from a marketing perspective, bridging the gap between pricing strategy and the customer-facing price points, tier names, and comparison tables that appear on the pricing page and in sales conversations.

## When to Use
- Pricing strategy has been approved and specific price points need to be locked before launch.
- A pricing page redesign is scheduled alongside a product or packaging change.
- A new tier or add-on is being introduced and needs to be slotted into the existing pricing framework.
- Finance has modelled unit economics but the customer-facing presentation has not been finalised.
- Sales reports that current pricing presentation creates confusion or friction in deal cycles.

## Workflow
1. Review the pricing strategy document. Confirm the strategic intent: value metric, competitive positioning (premium, parity, penetration), and target margin.
2. Review the packaging framework. Confirm tier names, feature allocation, and upgrade triggers are finalised.
3. Set specific price points for each tier. Align with the pricing strategy's willingness-to-pay data and competitive benchmarks. Use psychological pricing principles (e.g., anchoring, charm pricing) where appropriate.
4. Define add-on pricing for capabilities sold outside the core tiers. Ensure add-ons reinforce the upgrade path rather than cannibalising higher tiers.
5. Build the pricing page copy: tier names, one-line descriptions, price points, billing frequency options, feature comparison table, and CTA per tier.
6. Write the internal pricing reference sheet for sales: list prices, approved discount bands, deal desk escalation triggers, and competitive pricing intelligence.
7. Review with finance to confirm margin targets are met at each tier.
8. Review with sales leadership to confirm the pricing presentation matches how deals are actually structured and does not introduce friction.
9. Get final sign-off from pricing committee or leadership and lock the pricing for launch.

## Anti-Patterns
- **Finalising price points without referencing the pricing strategy.** *Why: Ad-hoc pricing decisions that ignore WTP data and competitive benchmarks lead to money left on the table or uncompetitive pricing.*
- **Publishing pricing without a sales reference sheet.** *Why: Reps need discount bands and escalation rules; without them, every deal becomes a one-off negotiation that erodes pricing integrity.*
- **Changing pricing after the enablement brief has been distributed.** *Why: Last-minute pricing changes force re-enablement and create inconsistency in early deals.*
- **Ignoring psychological pricing principles.** *Why: Buyer perception of value is influenced by anchoring and framing; ignoring these leads to suboptimal conversion even at the right price point.*

## Output

**Success:**
- Locked price points for all tiers and add-ons, approved by finance and sales leadership.
- Pricing page copy with tier descriptions, feature comparison, and CTAs ready for design.
- Internal pricing reference sheet distributed to sales before launch.

**Failure:**
- Price points finalised without finance sign-off, creating margin issues discovered post-launch.
- No internal reference sheet, leaving sales to improvise discount structures.
- Pricing locked so late that enablement materials must be updated after distribution.
