# Brand Voice Scoring Rubric

Reference rubric for evaluating brand voice consistency across content assets. Three dimensions are scored 0–10 each. Total score is the sum of all three dimensions (max 30).

## Score Interpretation

| Total Score | Rating | Recommendation |
|------------|--------|---------------|
| 27–30 | Excellent | Publish. On-brand across all dimensions. |
| 21–26 | Good | Minor revisions before publishing. |
| 14–20 | Needs Revision | Return to author with annotated corrections. |
| 8–13 | Off-Brand | Significant rewrite required before publication. |
| 0–7 | Critical Misalignment | Full redraft needed; do not publish. |

---

## Dimension 1: Tone Spectrum Adherence (0–10)

Tone spectrum defines the brand's personality range across a set of polarities. Each brand will have its own defined poles (e.g., confident-not-arrogant, direct-not-terse, warm-not-casual). Score based on how well the asset stays within the defined range.

### Generic Tone Polarity Reference

Most B2B brands operate on a subset of these polarities. Replace with your brand's specific definitions:

| Polarity | On-Brand Midpoint | Off-Brand Extremes |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| Confident ↔ Humble | States capability as fact; does not brag or hedge | Too aggressive ("We're the best"), too deferential ("We try to be...") |
| Direct ↔ Conversational | Gets to the point in 1–2 sentences; no filler | Too blunt (reads cold), too casual (reads unprofessional) |
| Expert ↔ Accessible | Uses precise terminology; explains it briefly | Too technical (alienates non-experts), too simplified (loses credibility) |
| Warm ↔ Professional | Treats the reader as a peer; respects their time | Too personal (reads as informal marketing), too corporate (reads as stiff) |
| Optimistic ↔ Realistic | Focuses on possibility; acknowledges challenge | Too aspirational (reads as hype), too cautionary (reads as pessimistic) |

### Tone Scoring

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Asset stays within all defined tone polarities throughout. No single sentence reads off-brand. Tone is consistent from opening to close. |
| 7–8 | Asset is generally on-brand but 1–3 sentences drift outside a polarity. Tone inconsistency is minor and does not define the asset's overall character. |
| 5–6 | Multiple sentences (4–8) fall outside tone polarities, or tone is clearly inconsistent in one section (e.g., on-brand intro, off-brand CTA). |
| 3–4 | Asset has a clear and persistent tone mismatch — consistently too formal, too casual, too aggressive, or too hedged throughout. |
| 1–2 | Asset reads like a different brand entirely. Tone is antithetical to the defined brand character. |
| 0 | No discernible brand tone. Generic, interchangeable, or AI-generated without refinement. |

**Annotation instruction**: Flag each off-brand sentence. Label the tone deviation type (too formal, too casual, too aggressive, too hedged, inconsistent) and suggest a rewrite in-line.

## Dimension 2: Vocabulary Alignment (0–10)

Vocabulary alignment measures how well the asset uses the brand's approved word list and avoids banned or deprecated terms.

### Vocabulary Categories

**Preferred terms**: Words and phrases the brand actively uses to describe itself, its product, and its market. Examples: product category name, positioning language, key differentiators as the brand frames them.

**Permitted terms**: Industry-standard language the brand accepts but does not own or emphasise.

**Avoided terms**: Words or phrases the brand has decided not to use — typically jargon, competitor terms, deprecated product names, buzzwords that have lost meaning, or language that conflicts with brand positioning.

**Banned terms**: Terms that must never appear in brand content — competitor product names used admiringly, legally sensitive language, deprecated names after a rebrand, or offensive language.

### Vocabulary Scoring

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Uses preferred vocabulary consistently. Zero banned terms. Zero avoided terms. Competitor references (if any) are neutral or brand-advantaged framings. |
| 7–8 | Primarily uses preferred vocabulary. 1–3 avoided terms present but none are banned. No competitor terms used disparagingly or in a way that could cause legal concern. |
| 5–6 | Mix of preferred and generic industry language. 4–6 avoided terms. No banned terms. |
| 3–4 | Predominant use of generic or competitor-derived language. 1–2 avoided terms that are significant enough to weaken brand positioning. |
| 1–2 | Asset uses a banned term, references a competitor product admiringly, or uses a deprecated product name prominently. |
| 0 | Asset is built around competitor framing, uses the brand's deprecated identity, or contains legally sensitive language. |

### Common Vocabulary Red Flags

Check each asset for:
- Product name accuracy: Is the product name spelled and capitalised exactly as defined in brand standards?
- Category framing: Does the asset use the brand's defined category language or a generic/competitor-defined alternative?
- Superlatives without proof: "Best", "leading", "fastest", "most powerful" without a citation or qualifier.
- Passive voice on claims: "It is believed that..." or "Customers have reported..." instead of owned claims with evidence.
- Hedge phrases that weaken positioning: "We try to...", "We hope to...", "In some cases..." when the brand should be stating capability as fact.

## Dimension 3: Clarity Score (0–10)

Clarity measures how easy the asset is to read and understand for the target audience. Clarity is not the same as simplicity — expert content can be clear; simple content can be unclear.

### Clarity Metrics

| Metric | Target | Tool |
|--------|--------|------|
| Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level | 8–12 for B2B; 6–9 for B2C | Hemingway App, Readable.com |
| Average sentence length | ≤ 20 words | Word processor or Hemingway App |
| Passive voice frequency | < 15% of sentences | Hemingway App, Grammarly |
| Unnecessary qualifiers | < 3 per 500 words | Manual review |
| Unexplained jargon count | 0 (all technical terms explained on first use) | Manual review |

### Clarity Scoring

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Reading level appropriate for the target audience. Average sentence ≤ 20 words. Passive voice < 10%. Zero unexplained jargon. Zero unnecessary qualifiers. |
| 7–8 | Reading level appropriate. Passive voice 10–15%. 1–3 sentences exceed 30 words. 1–2 unexplained technical terms. |
| 5–6 | Reading level slightly above target (Grade 13–15 for B2B). Passive voice 15–25%. Multiple long sentences (4–6 sentences > 30 words). |
| 3–4 | Dense paragraphs (5+ sentences without a line break). Passive voice > 25%. Multiple unexplained technical terms. |
| 1–2 | Asset is unreadable for the target audience. Reading level far above target. Dominated by passive voice and unexplained jargon. |
| 0 | Content is incoherent or untranslated from source language. |

### Clarity Rewrite Rules

Apply these rules in order when rewriting for clarity:

1. **One idea per sentence.** Split any sentence with two clauses joined by "and", "but", or "which" into two sentences.
2. **Kill the qualifiers.** Remove "very", "quite", "rather", "somewhat", "essentially", "basically" from every sentence.
3. **Activate the passive.** Replace "The report was generated by the system" with "The system generates the report."
4. **Front-load the benefit.** Move the outcome or value to the start of the sentence. "You can reduce churn by 30%" beats "By using our retention features, you can reduce churn by up to 30%."
5. **Explain or remove jargon.** On first use, follow technical terms with a plain-language parenthetical. Remove terms used for second use if the parenthetical is sufficient.

## Deviation Pattern Taxonomy

Use these labels when annotating content:

| Code | Deviation Type | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| T-FORMAL | Tone too formal for brand | "It is incumbent upon users to configure..." |
| T-CASUAL | Tone too casual for brand | "Hey there! Super excited to share..." |
| T-AGGRESS | Tone too aggressive or sales-heavy | "Don't miss out on this game-changing offer!" |
| T-HEDGE | Tone too hedged; lacks confidence | "We hope this might be useful for some teams..." |
| T-INCON | Tone inconsistent within single asset | Professional intro, casual outro |
| V-BANNED | Banned term used | Competitor name used admiringly |
| V-AVOID | Avoided term used | Deprecated product name |
| V-GENERIC | Generic language instead of brand preferred | "software" instead of brand category term |
| C-LONG | Sentence too long (> 30 words) | Long nested clause structure |
| C-PASSIVE | Passive voice | "The dashboard is updated by the system" |
| C-JARGON | Unexplained technical term | "Our API uses idempotent endpoints" with no explanation |
| C-QUALIFY | Unnecessary qualifier | "extremely powerful", "very fast" |
