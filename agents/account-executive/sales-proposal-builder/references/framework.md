# Framework: Sales Proposal Builder

This framework defines the 11-section proposal structure, ROI calculation methodology, pricing psychology principles, and competitive positioning strategies.

## 11-Section Proposal Structure

### 1. Cover Page
- Company logo, prospect logo, proposal title, date, version, AE contact information
- Confidentiality notice
- Guidance: clean, professional, minimal — the cover page sets the tone

### 2. Executive Summary
- 250 words maximum
- Lead with the prospect's business outcome, not product description
- Reference the prospect's own language from discovery
- End with the single most compelling reason to act now
- Guidance: the executive summary is the only page every stakeholder reads — optimize for decision-makers who will not read further

### 3. Current Situation
- Prospect's current state as confirmed through discovery
- Quantified cost of the problem (hours, dollars, risk, opportunity cost)
- Root causes identified during discovery
- Guidance: use the prospect's own words and data; this section proves you listened

### 4. Proposed Solution
- Solution overview mapped to stated requirements
- Requirements traceability matrix: requirement → capability → benefit
- Differentiating capabilities highlighted
- Partial-fit or workaround items flagged transparently
- Guidance: every capability mentioned must trace back to a stated requirement — orphan features weaken the proposal

### 5. ROI Analysis
- See ROI Calculation Methodology below
- Cost of status quo vs. investment vs. projected value
- Payback period and time-to-value
- Assumptions table with conservative/moderate/aggressive scenarios
- Guidance: always show your math; finance teams will check it

### 6. Competitive Comparison
- Structured as Why Us / Why Now / Why Not Alternatives
- Feature comparison table on evaluation criteria (not your full feature set)
- Proof points per claim (customer stories, benchmarks, analyst reports)
- Guidance: never attack competitors — position your strengths against their gaps

### 7. Implementation Plan
- Phased approach with milestones, durations, and dependencies
- Customer-side resource requirements and responsibilities
- Risk mitigation for common implementation challenges
- Path from contract signature to first value delivered
- Guidance: specificity signals competence; vague timelines signal risk

### 8. Team & Support
- Implementation team roles and qualifications
- Ongoing support model (SLA, channels, escalation path)
- Customer success assignment and cadence
- Guidance: name specific roles and describe what each does — "dedicated team" is meaningless without detail

### 9. Pricing Options
- See Pricing Psychology below
- Three-tier structure (Good / Better / Best)
- Per-tier: what is included, what is excluded, unit economics
- Volume discounts, multi-year incentives, payment terms
- Guidance: the middle option should be the most attractive for the prospect's stated needs

### 10. Terms
- Contract duration and renewal terms
- SLA commitments and remedies
- Data handling and security commitments
- Termination and migration assistance
- Guidance: keep terms simple and prospect-friendly; complex legal language creates friction

### 11. Next Steps
- Specific proposed timeline from proposal review to contract signature
- Mutual action plan with owner and due date per step
- AE contact information and availability
- Guidance: always propose a specific next meeting date — "let us know when you are ready" is a deal killer

## ROI Calculation Methodology

### Cost of Inaction (Annual)
Calculate what the problem costs the prospect annually:
- Direct costs: labor hours × loaded rate, tool costs, error remediation
- Indirect costs: opportunity cost, delayed revenue, compliance risk
- Growth penalty: how the problem scales with company growth

### Implementation Cost (One-Time + Recurring)
Total investment required:
- Software licensing (annual or multi-year)
- Implementation services
- Internal resource allocation (prospect-side)
- Training and change management
- Data migration

### Time-to-Value
- Days from contract signature to first measurable value
- Define "first value" concretely (e.g., "first automated report generated")
- Show early wins vs. full deployment timeline

### Payback Period
- Months until cumulative value exceeds cumulative investment
- Formula: Total Investment / Monthly Value Delivered
- Show break-even point on a chart if possible

### 3-Year TCO Comparison
Compare total cost of ownership across three scenarios:
1. Status quo (current costs projected forward, including growth penalty)
2. Your solution (implementation + licensing + internal resources)
3. Primary alternative (competitor pricing + implementation + switching costs)

### Sensitivity Analysis
Present three scenarios with clearly stated assumption differences:
- Conservative: lowest reasonable value, highest reasonable cost
- Moderate: most likely outcome based on comparable customer data
- Aggressive: best-case based on top-performing customer results

## Pricing Psychology

### Anchoring
Present the highest-value option first. The "Best" tier sets the price anchor, making the "Better" tier feel reasonable by comparison.

### Decoy Effect
Structure the "Good" tier to be clearly limited — missing 1-2 capabilities the prospect confirmed they need. This makes the "Better" tier the obvious rational choice.

### Good / Better / Best Structure

| Element | Good | Better | Best |
|---------|------|--------|------|
| Position | Entry point | Recommended | Premium |
| Target | Budget-constrained or pilot | Primary decision | Aspirational |
| Pricing | Base | 1.5-2x base | 2.5-3.5x base |
| Strategy | Solves core problem | Solves core + confirmed needs | Solves everything + future needs |

### Multi-Year Incentives
- Year 1: full price
- 2-year commit: 10-15% discount
- 3-year commit: 15-25% discount
- Guidance: never discount year 1 — it sets the wrong precedent

## Competitive Positioning Strategies

### Why Us
- Lead with capabilities that map to the prospect's requirements and that competitors cannot match
- Use proof points: named customers (with permission), quantified results, analyst recognition
- Never claim superiority in areas where competitors are genuinely stronger — it destroys credibility

### Why Now
- Quantify cost of delay: monthly cost of inaction × expected decision delay
- Reference time-sensitive factors: contract renewals, regulatory deadlines, growth milestones
- Show implementation timeline relative to prospect's target go-live date

### Why Not Alternatives
- Address specific competitors the prospect is evaluating (not the entire market)
- Focus on gaps relevant to this prospect's requirements — not generic weaknesses
- Use TCO comparison to shift the conversation from sticker price to total cost
- Never speak negatively about competitors — let the data tell the story
