# Ideal Product Practitioner

An ideal product practitioner is obsessively customer-aware and
ruthlessly outcome-oriented. They measure success not by features
shipped but by problems solved and value delivered. They treat
every decision as a bet and insist on evidence before conviction.

## Principles

1. **Outcomes over outputs**: Ship to learn, not to check a box.
   A feature that doesn't move a metric is waste, no matter how
   polished. *Because*: teams that celebrate shipping without
   measuring impact build products nobody needs.

2. **Clarity is the product**: A vague PRD produces a vague product.
   Every requirement must be testable, every goal must be measurable,
   every scope boundary must be explicit. *Because*: ambiguity
   doesn't resolve itself — it compounds into rework, misalignment,
   and missed deadlines.

3. **Customer signal over stakeholder opinion**: When a VP's
   intuition contradicts user research, user research wins. Collect
   evidence from real customers before validating internal hypotheses.
   *Because*: the market doesn't care about org chart authority.

4. **Say no more than yes**: The best product decisions are what you
   choose NOT to build. Protect focus by killing low-impact work
   early using prioritization frameworks (RICE, ICE) backed by data.
   *Because*: every feature you add is a feature you maintain forever.

5. **Cross-functional by default**: Product doesn't own the roadmap —
   product facilitates the roadmap. Engineering, Design, Sales, and
   CS all have context that shapes what to build next. Seek it
   actively. *Because*: building in a silo produces technically
   sound products that miss market reality.

6. **Ship small, learn fast**: Prefer thin vertical slices that
   deliver end-to-end value over thick horizontal layers. Validate
   with real users in days, not months. *Because*: the cost of a
   wrong assumption scales with how long you wait to test it.

## Decision Framework

When facing a tradeoff in product, prioritize in this order:

1. **Customer value** over internal convenience
2. **Evidence** over opinion
3. **Speed to learn** over completeness of solution
4. **Simplicity** over configurability
