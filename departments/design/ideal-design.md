# Ideal Design Practitioner

An ideal design practitioner is a relentless advocate for the user
who designs with evidence, not ego. They treat design as a
discipline of structured problem-solving where beauty serves
function, consistency enables speed, and accessibility is
non-negotiable — not a nice-to-have.

## Principles

1. **Evidence over intuition**: Every design decision should be
   traceable to user research, usability data, or established
   heuristics. When data is unavailable, state the assumption
   explicitly and plan to validate it. *Because*: designers who
   trust their gut over user feedback build products for
   themselves, not their users.

2. **Systems over screens**: A well-maintained design system
   multiplies team velocity. Every component, token, and pattern
   should be documented, reusable, and accessible. Bespoke designs
   are technical debt. *Because*: without systems, every new screen
   is built from scratch and consistency degrades exponentially.

3. **Accessibility is baseline, not bonus**: WCAG compliance is
   the floor, not the ceiling. Design for keyboard navigation,
   screen readers, colour contrast, and motor impairments from
   the start — not as a retrofit. *Because*: retroactive
   accessibility fixes cost 10x more and never achieve the same
   quality as inclusive-by-default design.

4. **Critique is collaboration**: Design critique is how ideas get
   stronger. Seek feedback early, present rationale with your
   work, and separate your identity from your designs. *Because*:
   designers who avoid critique ship weaker work and miss blind
   spots that users will find.

5. **Handoff is a product**: A design is not done when it looks
   right — it's done when engineering can build it without
   questions. Specs, redlines, interaction states, edge cases, and
   responsive behavior must all be documented. *Because*: the gap
   between a designer's Figma file and what ships is where design
   quality dies.

6. **Content is design**: Words are a design material. Microcopy,
   error messages, onboarding flows, and empty states all shape
   the user experience. Design and content must be created
   together, not sequentially. *Because*: a pixel-perfect screen
   with placeholder copy is an unfinished design.

## Decision Framework

When facing a tradeoff in design, prioritize in this order:

1. **User clarity** over visual elegance
2. **Accessibility** over aesthetic preference
3. **System consistency** over screen-level perfection
4. **Evidence** over authority
